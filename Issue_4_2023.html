Taking Stock of our Crops
With each new gardening season, we re-evaluate what we want to grow and where. Some of our crops—like our rhubarb and strawberry beds—we inherited when we took over the site in 2017. But in most other cases, we have considerable latitude. Considerations include robustness of soil in particular beds and amount of sunshine.

Over the next couple of issues of this newsletter we'll take a look at how a few of our key crops performed. We start with eggplant and carrots, both of which we gave new homes to this season.
Eggplants placed in black containers grew larger than those in the ground.
Container's warmer soil beneficial
The last couple of seasons that we've planted eggplant, the results were OK but not great. Some research suggested keeping the roots warmer could help—especially early in the season. After an initial period in pots, some of our eggplants were moved to black containers at the north end of the garden; the idea being the soil would be warmed by the sun. Others were placed at the edges of our Solanaceae beds (2 and 4).

Eggplants in the black containers developed more quickly as compared to those in the ground. As they matured, some were moved to our "D" sections to prevent overcrowding. As seen in the photo, those that stayed in the black containers grew very well. A shout-out to our volunteers that prepared the containers, which were fashioned from composters lined with landscape fabric clipped onto the top rim.
Raised bed's softer soil gave larger carrots.
Custom soil improves carrot yield
Like much of Toronto, the soil in Cedarvale Park has a deep layer of clay underneath. This poses a problem for our root vegetables—particularly carrots.

Thanks to a raised bed donated to the garden last fall, we now have a way to provide more suitable soil. Back in mid-April, we filled the raised bed with a mixture of soil and compost, let it settle for a week and then added more compost. We sowed carrot varieties Little Fingers, Sprint and Scarlet Nantes.
Overall, we've seen an improvement in length and diameter as compared to other locations for this crop, e.g., shed bed last year.
Carrots are likely to return to their loft apartment next year. We have tentative plans for a second raised bed.

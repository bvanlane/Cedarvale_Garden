  Hello Fellow Gardeners
Fall may soon be officially upon us, but our garden still thinks it’s summer. Thanks to all our efforts earlier in the season, we continue to take home some warm-weather crops. And through replanting, our harvesting pails still fill up with Swiss Chard, kale, arugula and other greens.
Just this past weekend we planted rapini!

And our herb bed? It’s still going strong.

Here’s a little sprig of seasoned humour to post for later inspiration: “Old gardeners never die, they just run out of thyme.”

  Looking Ahead
We continue to remove flowers from various brassicas, herbs and flowers. We still have a fair amount of compost in bin 2 to sift. This will be spread on our various beds before end-of-season, allowing time for the nutrients to work their way down into the soil.

Since our various insect garden companions haven’t yet given up on the season, we’ll likewise still pay attention to them.
Our senior gardeners will be debating the overall garden plan for next season, which we need to make decisions on now for determining where to do this fall’s planting for next season’s garlic.

And, on the more immediately satisfying side, we’ll still be harvesting our cool-weather crops for several more weeks to come.

Harvest Fest. Celebration, Sept. 24
Four shifts. One amazing garden team!

Each time we come to the garden, the fruits of others’ labour are on display. But with relatively few exceptions, we don’t often get to chat with those who plant, weed and water at different times than us. Our annual Harvest Festival is just such an opportunity.

We will be gathering this Sunday, September 24th at noon in the garden. If rain threatens the party, the alternate date is Sunday, Oct. 1.

Naturally part of our celebration will be enjoying tasty garden-inspired snacks! So please bring one along.
Delectable savoury items enjoyed in previous years are: tomato and basil on crackers, cucumber/tomato pesto bites, roasted garlic spread on toast, and zucchini bread. And of course, there's Nandini's Summer Squash Supreme from our Recipe Corner!

If sweet appeals to you, homemade fruit leather rolls and rhubarb squares have been crowd pleasers in the past. We’re no longer harvesting our own rhubarb, so store-bought rhubarb would have to suffice.

Let’s get together, enjoy each other’s company and celebrate our garden’s bounty!
Compost Moisture. Our bins need water added periodically. But how much? Here's a way to test if it's already moist enough: Scoop some out into a container, remove the sticks, grab a handful and squeeze—a few drops should come out. It should feel like a wrung-out sponge.

Much of our compost is green leaves, making it a more moist mixture; so we don't need to add all that much.

Harvesting Squash. Be on the lookout for yellowed, diseased foliage. We cut these off to prevent spread of mold. Be sure to cut the stem at the base where it meets the next stem. Observant gardeners have noticed left-over "stem stumps" getting moldy too. Leaves touching the ground should be cut off for the same reason.
Taking Stock of our Crops
With each new gardening season, we re-evaluate what we want to grow and where. Some of our crops—like our rhubarb and herb beds—we inherited when we took over the site in 2017. But in most other cases, we have considerable latitude. Considerations include suitability of soil in particular beds and amount of sunshine.

Over the next couple of issues of this newsletter we'll take a look at how a few of our key crops performed. We start with eggplant and carrots, both of which we gave new homes to this season.
Eggplants placed in black containers grew larger than those in the ground.
Container's warmer soil beneficial
Some research suggested keeping the roots of our eggplants warmer could improve production—especially helpful early in the season. After an initial period in pots, some of our eggplants were moved to black containers at the north end of the garden; the idea being the soil would be warmed by the sun. Others were placed at the edges of our Solanaceae beds (2 and 4).

Eggplants in the black containers developed more quickly as compared to those in the ground. A shout-out to our volunteers that prepared the containers, fashioned from low-cost composters. They inventively lined them with landscape fabric to cover the access holes at the bottom.
Raised bed's softer soil gave larger carrots.
Custom soil improves carrot yield
Like much of Toronto, the soil in Cedarvale Park has a deep layer of clay underneath. This poses a problem for our root vegetables—particularly carrots.

Thanks to a raised bed donated to the garden last fall, we now have a way to provide more suitable soil. Back in mid-April, we filled the raised bed with a mixture of soil and compost, let it settle for a week and then added more compost. We sowed carrot varieties Little Fingers, Sprint and Scarlet Nantes.
Overall, we've seen an improvement in length and diameter as compared to other locations for this crop, e.g., shed bed last year.
Carrots are likely to return to their loft apartment next year. We have tentative plans for a second raised bed.

Edible Weeds — New Series!
They're all around us. Whether we're working in our beloved garden, or walking past a vacant lot, many plants commonly called weeds are actually tasty, nutritional food sources.

The Newsletter Team is proud to announce a new series exploring these oft-disdained delicacies. Some of the plants we'll feature regularly find their way into our garden and/or the pollinator garden.
Other plants we'll profile sample our accommo-dations only occasionally—or can be expected to at some point. We begin with garlic mustard for the series. .
Garlic mustard is found in abundance in Cedarvale Ravine. This spring it had a massive showing in the Pollinator Garden, and is therefore of particular interest to us.
1st-year garlic mustard(left); at maturity(right)
Smells garlicky but isn’t garlic
Garlic mustard—an edible herb native to Europe—has turned aggressive forest invader, threatening biodiversity in many regions of Canada.

But did you know this invasive species is rather tasty? Can eating garlic mustard be a delicious way of getting rid of this stubborn plant? Let's give it a try! You can add garlic mustard to soups, use it as a pizza topping or make pesto out of it.
Identification
How can you identify garlic mustard in the garden?

First year garlic mustard is low-growing with dark green leaves that have scalloped margins and deep veins. A strong smell of garlic is present when the leaves are crushed.

Second year garlic mustard grows up to 1 m in height and has hairy stems, along with alternative, triangular, and coarsely toothed leaves.
In its second year the plant produces tiny white flowers in early May.

Be careful when trying to get rid of this weed. Improper harvesting can encourage growth by spreading seeds or disturbing the soil. Learn how to properly remove and dispose of garlic mustard: invasivespeciescentre.ca/invasive-species/meet-the-species/invasive-plants/garlic-mustard/

Summer Squash Supreme
This one’s pretty simple:
● cut squash into chunks; peel off skin if desired
● mix the chunks with salt, pepper, olive oil
● add a bit of paprika and turmeric to give it some colour (and additional flavour)
● bake in the oven for 30-ish minutes at 375 degrees Fahrenheit

Delicious with a fresh, light taste!

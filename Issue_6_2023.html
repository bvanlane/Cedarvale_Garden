Rhubarb research

 Over time, the advice on how to handle inedible portions of crops can evolve. This is the case with rhubarb. Based on more recent research, our senior gardeners believe it's safe for us to compost our rhubarb leaves with the rest of the organic material, rather than using a different bin (the black one, close to the shed bed entrance). The poisonous oxalic acid in the leaves breaks down during the composting process.

 We'll be protecting the crowns of the rhubarb once the leaves die away as the colder weather sets in. Mulch will go around them, with a layer of straw covering them.
  Cover Crops
Building capacity. This is a popular term used throughout the public and private sector these days. But in gardening, it's long been seen as vital. Planting cover crops is one way we "amend" the soil. With these plants, we're not intending to harvest them, but rather we're taking advantage of special properties of their leaves and roots to leave the soil richer than before.

This year, we're planting Red Clover and Rye Grass for this purpose. The Red Clover has a deep tap root that increases soil permeability and brings nutrients up from the sub soil. It also fixes nitrogen from the air, adding it to the soil through its roots.
Rye Grass prevents soil erosion that can take place in early spring with melting snow and heavy rains. The nitrogen its roots take in from the soil is kept from leaching away.

Both plants add organic matter when they decompose.

We're sowing their seeds in various places throughout the garden. So far: Bed 1, "D" section; Bed 2, middle sections; Bed 3, middle sections and "D" sections; and Fence Bed, between chives and calaloo.

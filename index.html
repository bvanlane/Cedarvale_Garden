Three sisters birth new epoch
Crawford Lake boasts one of the earliest known sites of companion planting in Ontario, dating back to the 1400s. Its bragging rights will now have even deeper roots: it outcompeted 11 other archaeological sites across the world as being the best indicator of the Anthropocene, a proposed new geologic epoch.

The two honours are related as follows. The evidence for the early cultivation of corn, beans and squash (Three Sisters) at Crawford Lake is as a result of the lake's unique process of sediment formation, discovered in the early '70s. Alternating layers of calcium carbonate and organic material settle out each succeeding summer and winter, respectively. This allows precise dating of deposits. The concentration of maize pollen preserved in the lake sediment suggested nearby 15th century agriculture — an Iroquois village, it turned out.

Borage – It’s a question of balance
Much of what we attempt to do in the garden is create optimum conditions. But as the season progresses and our various crops develop, we’re constantly tweaking our actions/interventions.

“The balance of nature is not a status quo; it is fluid, ever shifting, in a constant state of adjustment” — anti-pesticide crusader Rachel Carson*, from seminal book Silent Spring (1962).

So it is in our garden.

Our borage is a case in point. It made its debut in our strawberry patch last season as a companion plant. We started it from seeds, and then it self-seeded. Very successfully. Maybe a bit too successfully. There are indeed multiple benefits to having borage in our strawberry patch. And elsewhere, too. For example, in our fence bed.

But, there can be too much of a good thing. The borage was starting to compete with the strawberries, our senior gardeners concluded. So, we took it back a bit.
Borage in fence bed to recondition soil
And just what are the benefits of borage? Below are some of the many:
● repels some pests
● its purple/blue flowers are particularly attractive to bees
● their simple structure makes it easy for pollinators to access the nectar
● it blooms over a long season, which is helpful for our ongoing strawberry crop
● aphids are attracted to it, helping it draw away this pest from crops like our brassicas
● as a cover crop it captures and stores nutrients such as potassium, and fixes nitrogen from the air and stores it in its root nodules
● its root structure penetrates compacted soil and helps aerate it.

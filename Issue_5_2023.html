  Hello Fellow Gardeners
In recent years, media attention around Thanksgiving has broadened. Commentators, hosts and guests still reference turkeys—and food in general—but often express gratitude in their lives for people and relationships too.

We in this garden have a wonderful combination of a close connection to some of the food that we eat, as well as relationships with those with whom we share its production.
Our cooperation in this garden is something to be very proud of.

As well, the opportunity we have to be part of this relatively rare model is indeed something to have gratitude for.

May we all have a wonderful Thanksgiving, both as givers and receivers of blessings.

  Looking Ahead
We’re now entering the last week or two of harvesting.

Please pick what is available of our hot-weather plants, e.g., squash, peppers, tomatoes and beans. Apparently Brussels sprouts benefit from a frost, so we’ll be keeping them in for a while longer.

Once all the remaining crops have been harvested, we’ll be spreading compost.
This is the ideal time for amending our soil, as the beds can be too soggy early in the spring.

As a pleasant reminder of the spring—that always does come, even though winter can seem to last forever—we’ll be planting next season’s garlic. It will be covered with straw to provide some protection from the cold. We’ll also mulch some of our perennials.
  Harvest Festival Report
It was sunny. It felt warm. And outside the garden gate, the same was true.

It was our garden's annual Harvest Festival, held September 24th this year, and the atmosphere was celebratory.

Talk was naturally of the garden variety: "Weren’t the radishes extra hot this year!" And, "Yeah, weren’t the tomatoes extra flavourful too—especially the little ones! Not many split ones either." It was a chance to renew acquaintance with friends on different shifts, and everyone made the most of it.

This year, we held the event inside the garden, pressing the grey storage box into service for snacks.
The lid’s normally drab surface became splashed with the bright red of cherry tomato slices, bedded on basil leaves nestled on tiny crackers. From this newsletter’s Recipe Corner, cucumber coolness sparred with Thai chili heat, contested in a wading pool of yogurt; our palates were the winners! Garden garlic gave a strong showing in a creamy hummus. Sweet tooths were satisfied by almond biscotti cookies. Thanks go to all who brought those and other delectable offerings.

Our Sunday shift had kindly watered and weeded beforehand, so there was little to do afterwards but close and lock the gate. And, maybe think a bit wistfully of how the garden season is drawing to a close, anticipating the next time we can all gather together again.
Compost: What's in, what's out Some seeds are remarkably durable. Those of purslane, borage and oregano rank up there with berry seeds that withstand the gastrointestinal tract gauntlet of birds, bears and rodents. We've come to believe the aforementioned seeds are more than a match for our compost decomposition, and find their way uninvited into our beds. We're now putting these culled plants in the black rhubarb composter.

 Our crops' parting gifts—their roots Even after the last beans are plucked, all the tomatoes taken and the remaining squash shared, there is more our crops can give.

By leaving the roots in the ground, we let the ensuing decomposition fertilize our soil for next season. We've already started doing that with crops such as lettuce that won't withstand the first frost.
Bean and Squash combo
Our yields from these crops were pretty good this year. In fact, we could have harvested a few more of the pole beans; it can actually be quite difficult to spot mature pods amongst the tangle of leaves. From member reports on the acorn squash, they were firm and flavourful.

A garden guest wearing a bunny suit couldn't wait for our beans to mature. While they were only a few inches tall, sharp incisors nipped off the nascent vines. As a precaution, next season we can wrap mesh around the tepees' base at the time the beans go in. The squash follow about a week or so later.

Lamb's-quarters—incipient flowering
Credit: Gov. of Ont.
Lamb’s-quarters: Not so baahd at all!
Lamb’s-quarters is one of the most common weeds found in Ontario. In 2016 it ranked as Ontario’s worst weed in a University of Guelph survey!

While lamb's-quarters is often considered among the most troublesome weeds, its low-life reputation is quite undeserved. Young lamb’s-quarters can be eaten raw or cooked like spinach and is extremely nutritious. In fact, it is quite popular in many cuisines!

If you think you may have some lamb’s-quarters taking residency in your own garden, be aware its cotyledons and those of spinach look a lot alike. So be extra careful when weeding them. What are cotyledons, you ask? These are leaves that first appear from a germinating seed.
How can you distinguish lamb’s-quarters from spinach? Spinach has narrower cotyledons.

Interested in giving lamb's-quarters a try? Check out this helpful guide: foodprint.org/real-food/lambs-quarters/

Nasturtium Capers
Ingredients:
● 1c nasturtium seeds
● 1c white vinegar
● 1 tsp salt
● 5 - 8 peppercorns, slightly crushed
● 1 clove garlic, crushed
● a few small sprigs of fresh thyme
● 1 bay leaf

Method:
● 1 - rinse with water and drain seeds; blot dry
● 2 - place into a clean, 1-pint canning jar
● 3 - bring vinegar, salt, pepper to a boil; pour into the jar
● 4 - add garlic, thyme, bay leaf
● 5 - seal and refrigerate for at least 3 months

A big shout-out to Janet for sharing her tried-and-true recipe.

Let's have some ado about Calaloo
Our calaloo is a variety of amaranthus

T his garden crop would make a great multiple-choice question in a culinary exam.

Is calaloo:
● a plant harvested for its leaves?
● a dish popular throughout Caribbean cuisine?
● a plant whose seeds are cooked as alternatives to quinoa, rice or couscous?
● spelt at least six different ways?

The correct answer is ... all of the above!

Calaloo is actually a term commonly used for many species of edible greens. These include multiple varieties of amaranthus (our plants are one of these), taro/dasheen, tannia/malanga, pokeweed and water spinach.
Some seeds in Ileana's palm she extracted

As a dish, depending upon the Caribbean country, one or more of the species mentioned above is either steamed with a variety of other ingredients, or slowly cooked in a pot into a kind of stew along with other vegetables and sometimes with meat or fish.

The seeds have a nutty flavour, making them an interesting substitute for various other grains, as was recently drawn to our attention by Ileana. Marta notes they can be extracted from their pods by shaking in a paper bag, once the pods have completely dried out. The seeds of aramanthus plants are known as amaranth, naturally enough.

With a large Caribbean diaspora, Greater Toronto Area generates enough demand for calaloo to support a local farm specializing in supplying the tannia/malanga species of the plant. See freshcallaloo.com/
  A special thanks
Being a member of the garden's watering committee is a bit like being on call. Perhaps in reverse, though. These volunteers set aside a part of their day—numerous times per month—for a duty they may not need to fulfill. Weather forecasts are becoming increasingly unreliable except for short lead times, so any relief unexpected rain brings comes very late.

And yet, watering is a critical part of making a garden grow.
